Awaaz IIT Kharagpur

The Indian Institutes of Technology (IITs) have sought Rs. 22,000 crore from the Ministry of Human Resource Development that is needed to spend over the next five years to improve their infrastructure and rise in global rankings. QS World University Rankings for 2016-17 had seen six of the IITs slump down from their positions. The money has been sought as part of Project Vishwajeet, which HRD Ministry wants to implement in order to make the IITs reach within the ranks of the top 100 universities in the world by 2018 and within the top 50 by 2020.
The IITs targeted are Delhi, Bombay, Kanpur, Kharagpur, Madras, Roorkee and Guwahati. Of the nearly Rs 22,000 crore, IIT-Kharagpur has sought Rs 3,700 crore, IIT-Bombay Rs 2,250 crore, IIT-Delhi Rs 2,400 crore, IIT-Kanpur Rs 2,475 crore, IIT-Madras Rs 2,800 crore, IIT-Guwahati Rs 2,695 crore and IIT-Roorkee Rs 5,550 crore. The money will go into improving "research output, faculty, infrastructure and international outlook."

The word career means a person's "course or progress through life". It has proven itself to be more complex and overloaded than string theory and sure has taken away the sleep of many young people.

Carrying forward the series 'aap ki raay', the results of the third poll showcase the factors prioritized by students while choosing a career. Evidently, a lion's share of the students aren't sure about their career goals and are still exploring the possible options. A little more than one-sixth felt that running after money is the need of the hour and is fairly justified to do so.

About 18% are following their interests, ignoring factors such as job security and others whereas about 7% are blindly following the herd. Just the 1% are working on their own startup ideas and have opted out of campus placements. Another 8% have planned for higher studies including options like an MS or a PhD degree, IAS, etc. and have as well opted out of campus placements. More than 17% have an interest in two of the latter options but would still like to keep placements from the campus as a backup.

Carbon dioxide which may be a dangerous waste for the planet is a vital input for Tuticorin Alkali Chemicals for making soda ash. They are unable to get enough carbon dioxide which has been resolved by a six-year-old startup, Carbon Clean Solutions which was set up by two undergraduate students in 2009. The startup meant at extracting carbon dioxide from flue gases in power plants and use it to make valuable products and even the CEO of Carbon Cleans Solutions said: “We are innovating the carbon dioxide capture so that its price is no more a constraint for conversion to products.” There are about 22 plants which have the capacity to capture around 40million tonnes per year, according to Global CSS Institute, an International member driven organisation. Aniruddha Sharma and Prateek Bumb started this startup and because of their hard work, they are able to develop proprietary solvent which could dissolve carbon dioxide and after testing, it came out that it could remove 90% carbon dioxide from fuel gases. Finally, they got Tuticorin Alkali Chemicals as their customer which is a big breakthrough for the company.

Prof M A Rege, ex-HoD Department of Architecture & Regional Planning, IIT Kharagpur is no more. Fondly remembered by many students and admirers.

Amherst - Madhukar Anant Rege passed away at age 92, on September 20th, 2016, after a long illness and a long, full life on three continents.

Born in Ratnagiri on the Konkan Coast of India, he had a happy, active childhood. From an early age he loved drawing and painting and eventually earned a Diploma in Architecture at the J.J. School of Art in Bombay, after apprenticing for a year with the master watercolorist Sawlaram Haldankar. In 1949, at age 25, he set sail for England with the help of his eldest brother Vinayak, where he successfully sat the R.I.B.A. (Royal Institute of British Architects) examination and then completed a post-graduate Diploma in Town Planning.

It was in England that he met and married Gladys Sharp on July 4th, 1953, and, after the birth of their first daughter in 1954, returned to India, where he took up a lectureship in Architecture at the newly formed Indian Institute of Technology in Kharagpur, West Bengal. He was to stay at I.I.T. for 14 years, aside from a year's leave in London and a three-year period in Athens, Greece where he worked with the pioneering Doxiadis Associates, after which he returned to Kharagpur as Professor and Head of the Department of Architecture and City Planning.

In 1969 he migrated to the United States, first as a SPURS (Special Program for Urban and Regional Studies) Fellow at M.I.T. in Cambridge, Massachusetts, then as a city planner for the City of Newton and eventually as a planner for the Massachusetts Executive Office of Communities and Development, where he worked in the Main Street Program on downtown revitalization of Mass. cities and towns. He also lectured at the Boston Architectural Center. When his wife and daughters joined him in 1970, he moved first to Brookline, then to Newton, and, upon retirement in 1993, settled with Gladys in Amherst. After retirement he returned to his first passion, painting, which he had kept up throughout his life, filling his home with beauty.

He was a true Renaissance man: a lifelong reader, always with his head in a book, but also an avid gardener. For many years he practiced yoga regularly. He made toys for his children and most of the furniture for his family, taught himself leatherwork, loved philosophical and political discussions, music, swimming, tennis, and cooking, and was a staunch Democrat, making sure to vote, by absentee ballot, even during his last illness.

He leaves behind Gladys, his loving wife of 63 years; daughters Josna Rege, in Amherst and Sally Rege Carroll, in North Reading, Massachusetts; grandsons Nikhil, in New York City, and Tyler, also from North Reading, currently studying at UMass Amherst; sisters Kumud Rege and Sunita Kulkarni, and brothers Prabhakar and Sadanand Rege, all in Mumbai, India; sons-in-law Andrew and Kevin, many loving nieces and nephews, brothers- and sisters-in law, and fast friends in India, England, and the United States. A great loss to us all, but a life well lived.

Beers and Story Belchertown Funeral Home has been entrusted with the arrangements.
